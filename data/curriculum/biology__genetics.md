# Genetics

## DNA Structure
DNA (deoxyribonucleic acid) is the molecule that carries genetic information in all living organisms.

### Double Helix
DNA consists of two strands wound around each other in a **double helix**, discovered by Watson and Crick in 1953 (based on Franklin's X-ray crystallography data).

### Nucleotides
Each strand is made of **nucleotides**, each containing:
- A deoxyribose sugar
- A phosphate group
- One of four nitrogenous bases: **Adenine (A), Thymine (T), Guanine (G), Cytosine (C)**

### Base Pairing Rules
The two strands are held together by hydrogen bonds between complementary bases:
- A pairs with T (2 hydrogen bonds)
- G pairs with C (3 hydrogen bonds)

This **complementary base pairing** is the key to DNA replication and transcription.

## DNA Replication
Before cell division, DNA must be copied so each daughter cell receives a complete genome.

### Process (Semi-conservative replication):
1. **Helicase** unwinds and separates the double helix at the **origin of replication**
2. **Primase** adds short RNA primers to provide a starting point
3. **DNA Polymerase III** reads the template strand (3'→5') and adds complementary nucleotides (5'→3')
4. **Leading strand** is synthesised continuously; **lagging strand** is synthesised in fragments (**Okazaki fragments**)
5. **DNA Polymerase I** removes RNA primers and replaces with DNA
6. **DNA Ligase** joins Okazaki fragments together

Result: two identical DNA double helices, each with one original and one new strand (semi-conservative).

## Gene Expression

### What is a Gene?
A gene is a sequence of DNA that codes for a functional product — usually a protein. Humans have approximately 20,000-25,000 genes.

### Transcription (DNA → mRNA)
Occurs in the nucleus.
1. **RNA Polymerase** binds to the promoter region of a gene
2. It unwinds DNA and reads the template strand (3'→5')
3. Builds a complementary mRNA strand using RNA nucleotides (U replaces T)
4. The mRNA transcript undergoes **RNA processing**: 5' cap added, poly-A tail added, **introns** (non-coding sequences) spliced out, **exons** (coding sequences) joined
5. Mature mRNA exits through nuclear pores to the cytoplasm

### Translation (mRNA → Protein)
Occurs at ribosomes in the cytoplasm.
1. Ribosome binds to mRNA at the **start codon** (AUG)
2. **tRNA** molecules carry specific amino acids; each tRNA has an **anticodon** complementary to an mRNA codon
3. Ribosome reads mRNA codons (triplets of 3 bases) and catalyses peptide bonds between amino acids
4. Chain grows until a **stop codon** (UAA, UAG, UGA) is reached
5. Protein is released and folds into its functional shape

### The Genetic Code
- 64 possible codons (4³), coding for 20 amino acids
- The code is **degenerate** — multiple codons can code for the same amino acid
- The code is **universal** — nearly the same in all organisms

## Inheritance Patterns

### Mendel's Laws
Gregor Mendel discovered the principles of inheritance through pea plant experiments:

**Law of Segregation** — each organism carries two alleles for each trait; these separate during gamete formation so each gamete carries only one allele

**Law of Independent Assortment** — alleles of different genes are distributed to gametes independently (applies when genes are on different chromosomes)

### Key Terms
- **Allele** — a variant of a gene (e.g., tall/short, brown eyes/blue eyes)
- **Homozygous** — two identical alleles (AA or aa)
- **Heterozygous** — two different alleles (Aa)
- **Dominant** — allele expressed when one or two copies are present (A)
- **Recessive** — allele only expressed when two copies are present (aa)
- **Genotype** — genetic makeup (e.g., Aa)
- **Phenotype** — observable trait (e.g., tall)

### Punnett Squares
Used to predict offspring genotype ratios. For a cross between two heterozygotes (Aa × Aa):
- 1/4 AA (homozygous dominant)
- 2/4 Aa (heterozygous)
- 1/4 aa (homozygous recessive)
- Phenotype ratio: 3 dominant : 1 recessive

### Beyond Simple Dominance
- **Incomplete dominance** — heterozygote shows intermediate phenotype (e.g., red × white = pink)
- **Codominance** — both alleles expressed simultaneously (e.g., AB blood type)
- **Sex-linked traits** — genes on X chromosome; more common in males (e.g., colour blindness, haemophilia)
- **Polygenic traits** — multiple genes contribute to one trait (e.g., height, skin colour)

## Mutations
A mutation is a change in the DNA sequence.

### Types:
- **Point mutation** — one base substituted for another
  - Silent: no change in amino acid
  - Missense: different amino acid → altered protein
  - Nonsense: creates stop codon → truncated protein
- **Frameshift mutation** — insertion or deletion of bases shifts the reading frame, altering all downstream codons
- **Chromosomal mutation** — large-scale changes: deletion, duplication, inversion, translocation

### Causes:
- Spontaneous replication errors
- Mutagens: UV radiation, X-rays, chemical carcinogens

### Effects:
- Most mutations are neutral or harmful
- Rare beneficial mutations are the raw material for evolution by natural selection
