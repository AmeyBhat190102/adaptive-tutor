# Newton's Laws of Motion

## Introduction
Isaac Newton published his three laws of motion in 1687 in his work *Principia Mathematica*. These laws form the foundation of classical mechanics and describe how objects move when forces act on them.

## Newton's First Law — Law of Inertia

**Statement:** An object at rest stays at rest, and an object in motion stays in motion at constant velocity, unless acted upon by a net external force.

### Key Concept: Inertia
**Inertia** is the tendency of an object to resist changes to its state of motion. Mass is a measure of inertia — the greater the mass, the greater the inertia.

### Implications
- A stationary book stays still unless pushed
- A sliding hockey puck would continue forever if there were no friction
- Passengers lurch forward when a car brakes suddenly (their bodies want to continue moving)
- Seatbelts protect passengers by providing a force to stop them when the car decelerates

### Equilibrium
An object is in equilibrium when the net force on it is zero. It can be:
- **Static equilibrium** — at rest (e.g., a book on a table)
- **Dynamic equilibrium** — moving at constant velocity (e.g., car at constant speed on a straight road)

## Newton's Second Law — Law of Acceleration

**Statement:** The acceleration of an object is directly proportional to the net force acting on it and inversely proportional to its mass.

**Equation: F = ma**

Where:
- F = net force (Newtons, N)
- m = mass (kilograms, kg)
- a = acceleration (metres per second squared, m/s²)

### Important Distinctions
- **Mass** is the amount of matter in an object (kg) — constant everywhere
- **Weight** is the gravitational force on an object: W = mg, where g = 9.8 m/s² on Earth's surface
- A 10 kg object weighs 98 N on Earth, but only 16.3 N on the Moon (g_moon ≈ 1.63 m/s²)

### Worked Examples

**Example 1:** A 5 kg box is pushed with a net force of 20 N. What is its acceleration?
a = F/m = 20/5 = 4 m/s²

**Example 2:** What force is needed to accelerate a 1200 kg car at 3 m/s²?
F = ma = 1200 × 3 = 3600 N

**Example 3 (multiple forces):** A 3 kg object has a 15 N force to the right and a 6 N friction force to the left.
Net force = 15 - 6 = 9 N (to the right)
a = F/m = 9/3 = 3 m/s² (to the right)

### Free Body Diagrams
A free body diagram shows all forces acting on an object as arrows:
- Length of arrow represents magnitude
- Direction of arrow represents direction of force
- Label each force (weight, normal force, tension, friction, applied force)

## Newton's Third Law — Law of Action-Reaction

**Statement:** For every action, there is an equal and opposite reaction.

More precisely: When object A exerts a force on object B, object B exerts a force on object A that is equal in magnitude but opposite in direction.

**F_AB = -F_BA**

### Key Points
- Action-reaction pairs always act on **different objects**
- They are always equal in magnitude and opposite in direction
- They do NOT cancel each other out (they act on different objects)

### Examples
- Walking: you push backward on the ground; the ground pushes you forward
- Rocket propulsion: exhaust gases pushed backward; rocket pushed forward
- Swimming: you push water backward; water pushes you forward
- A gun and bullet: gun pushes bullet forward; bullet pushes gun backward (recoil)

### Common Misconception
Students often think action-reaction forces cancel. They don't — they act on different objects. The forces that cancel are when two forces act on the **same** object and are equal and opposite (equilibrium).

## Applications of Newton's Laws

### Friction
Friction is a force that opposes relative motion between surfaces.
- **Static friction** — prevents motion from starting; can vary from 0 to a maximum value
- **Kinetic friction** — acts while objects are sliding; roughly constant

f = μN, where μ is the coefficient of friction and N is the normal force

### Tension
Force transmitted through a rope, string, or cable. In a massless rope, tension is the same throughout.

### Inclined Planes
For an object on a ramp at angle θ:
- Component of weight along the ramp: mg sin θ
- Component of weight perpendicular to ramp: mg cos θ
- Normal force: N = mg cos θ

### Circular Motion
For an object moving in a circle, there must be a **centripetal force** directed toward the centre:
F_c = mv²/r

This is not a new type of force — it is provided by existing forces (gravity for orbits, tension for a spinning ball, friction for a car turning a corner).

## Kinematics (Motion Equations)
When acceleration is constant, use the SUVAT equations:

- v = u + at
- s = ut + ½at²
- v² = u² + 2as
- s = (u + v)t / 2

Where: s = displacement, u = initial velocity, v = final velocity, a = acceleration, t = time
