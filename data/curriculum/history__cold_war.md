# The Cold War (1947–1991)

## What Was the Cold War?
The Cold War was a period of geopolitical tension between the United States (and its Western allies) and the Soviet Union (and its Eastern bloc allies) following World War II. It was "cold" because the two superpowers never directly fought each other in open warfare — instead competing through proxy wars, arms races, space exploration, and ideological struggle.

## Origins

### Ideological Conflict
The US and Soviet Union had fundamentally opposed systems:
- **USA:** capitalist democracy, free markets, individual liberty
- **USSR:** communist state, centrally planned economy, collective over individual

### Post-War Power Vacuum
WWII destroyed the old European powers. The US and USSR emerged as the two dominant superpowers, each with a sphere of influence and competing visions for the post-war world.

### Early Tensions
- **Yalta Conference (1945):** Roosevelt, Churchill, and Stalin agreed to divide Europe into spheres of influence but disagreed on Poland and Eastern Europe
- **Soviet expansion:** USSR installed communist governments across Eastern Europe by 1947 — Poland, Czechoslovakia, Hungary, Romania, Bulgaria, East Germany
- **Iron Curtain:** Churchill's 1946 speech describing the division of Europe between free West and Soviet-dominated East

## Key Doctrines and Policies

### Truman Doctrine (1947)
US would provide political, military, and economic support to any nation threatened by communist expansion. Announced in response to communist threats in Greece and Turkey.

### Marshall Plan (1948)
US provided $13 billion in economic aid to rebuild Western European economies after WWII — both humanitarian and strategic, as prosperous democracies were less likely to turn communist.

### Containment
George Kennan's strategy: don't try to roll back communism where it exists, but prevent it from spreading further. Became the foundation of US Cold War strategy.

### NATO (1949)
North Atlantic Treaty Organization — military alliance of Western nations. If one member was attacked, all would respond. US commitment to defend Western Europe.

### Warsaw Pact (1955)
Soviet-led military alliance of Eastern Bloc nations — Soviet response to NATO and West Germany joining NATO.

## Major Events

### Berlin Blockade (1948-49)
USSR blockaded West Berlin (inside East Germany). US and allies mounted the Berlin Airlift — flew in supplies for 11 months. USSR backed down. Showed Western resolve.

### Korean War (1950-53)
North Korea (Soviet/Chinese backed) invaded South Korea (US/UN backed). First major proxy war of the Cold War. Ended in stalemate at roughly the original border (38th parallel). 36,000 US dead.

### Arms Race
- USSR tested its first atomic bomb (1949)
- Both sides developed hydrogen bombs (1952-53)
- **Mutually Assured Destruction (MAD):** both sides had enough weapons to destroy each other; nuclear war became unthinkable
- Arms race extended to missiles (ICBMs) and submarine-launched missiles

### Space Race
- USSR launched Sputnik (1957) — first satellite; demonstrated ICBM capability
- Yuri Gagarin — first human in space (1961)
- US landed on the Moon (1969) — symbolic victory for the West

### Cuban Missile Crisis (1962)
The closest the Cold War came to nuclear war:
- US discovered Soviet nuclear missiles being installed in Cuba
- 13-day standoff; Kennedy blockaded Cuba; demanded missiles removed
- Soviets agreed to remove missiles; US secretly agreed not to invade Cuba and to remove missiles from Turkey
- Led to the Moscow-Washington hotline ("red phone") and partial nuclear test ban treaty

### Vietnam War (1955-75)
US supported South Vietnam against communist North Vietnam. US military involvement escalated to 500,000+ troops. Over 58,000 US and millions of Vietnamese died. North Vietnam ultimately unified the country under communism (1975) — first major US Cold War defeat.

### Détente (1970s)
Period of reduced tensions:
- Nixon visited China (1972) — began normalisation of US-China relations
- SALT I Treaty (1972) — first arms control agreement limiting nuclear weapons
- Helsinki Accords (1975) — recognised post-WWII European borders; included human rights provisions

## The End of the Cold War

### Reagan and the "Evil Empire"
US President Reagan (1981-89) massively increased defence spending and directly challenged Soviet ideology, calling USSR an "evil empire." The Strategic Defense Initiative (SDI/"Star Wars") threatened to make Soviet missiles obsolete.

### Gorbachev's Reforms
Soviet leader Mikhail Gorbachev introduced:
- **Glasnost** (openness) — greater freedom of speech and press
- **Perestroika** (restructuring) — economic reforms to modernise Soviet economy
These reforms unleashed forces Gorbachev could not control.

### Fall of the Berlin Wall (9 November 1989)
East Germany opened its borders. Crowds tore down the Berlin Wall — the most powerful symbol of Cold War division. Germany reunified in 1990.

### Collapse of the Soviet Union (1991)
Soviet republics declared independence. The Communist Party was banned after a failed coup. On 25 December 1991, Gorbachev resigned and the Soviet Union officially dissolved into 15 independent states.

## Legacy
- The United States emerged as the world's sole superpower
- NATO expanded eastward, incorporating former Soviet bloc countries
- Nuclear weapons remain a global concern
- Many conflicts today have roots in Cold War proxy wars (Afghanistan, Korea, parts of Africa)
- The ideological debate between liberal democracy and authoritarianism continues
