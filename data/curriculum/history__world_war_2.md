# World War II (1939–1945)

## Causes of World War II

### The Legacy of World War I
The Treaty of Versailles (1919) imposed harsh conditions on Germany:
- War guilt clause (Article 231) blamed Germany entirely for WWI
- Reparations of 132 billion gold marks
- Loss of 13% of territory and 10% of population
- Military severely restricted (100,000 troops, no air force)

These humiliating terms created deep resentment in Germany and economic hardship, creating fertile ground for extremist politics.

### The Rise of Fascism
The interwar period saw the rise of authoritarian, nationalist movements:
- **Benito Mussolini** took power in Italy (1922), creating the first fascist state
- **Adolf Hitler** and the National Socialist (Nazi) Party rose to power in Germany (1933)
- Hitler rebuilt the German military in violation of Versailles, annexed Austria (1938), and seized the Sudetenland
- **Military expansionism** in Japan under increasingly militaristic governments

### The Great Depression
The global economic collapse after 1929 caused mass unemployment and political instability. It weakened democratic governments and strengthened extremist parties who promised strong leadership and national revival.

### Appeasement
Britain and France attempted to satisfy Hitler's territorial demands to avoid war. The **Munich Agreement (1938)** allowed Germany to annex the Sudetenland in exchange for Hitler's promise of no further expansion. This emboldened Hitler rather than satisfying him.

### Failure of Collective Security
The **League of Nations** — established after WWI to maintain peace — proved ineffective:
- The United States never joined
- It had no military force
- Major powers ignored its resolutions (Japan invaded Manchuria 1931; Italy invaded Ethiopia 1935)

## The War in Europe

### The Invasion of Poland (1 September 1939)
Germany invaded Poland using **Blitzkrieg** (lightning war) — fast, coordinated attacks combining tanks, motorised infantry, and air power. Britain and France declared war on Germany (3 September 1939).

### Fall of France (1940)
Germany invaded France through the Ardennes forest, bypassing the Maginot Line. France fell in six weeks. British and Allied forces were evacuated from **Dunkirk** (338,000 troops saved by a fleet of military and civilian vessels).

### The Battle of Britain (1940)
Germany attempted to destroy the Royal Air Force to enable an invasion of Britain. The RAF, aided by radar and the code-breaking efforts at Bletchley Park, defeated the Luftwaffe. This was Hitler's first major defeat.

### Operation Barbarossa (June 1941)
Germany invaded the Soviet Union — the largest military operation in history. Initial German advances were rapid, but the Soviet Union's vast territory, harsh winters, and determined resistance eventually stalled and reversed the German advance.

### The Holocaust
Nazi Germany systematically murdered approximately **6 million Jews** and 5-6 million others (Roma, disabled people, political opponents, homosexuals, Soviet prisoners of war) in the **Holocaust**. The systematic murder used concentration camps, death camps (Auschwitz, Treblinka, Sobibor), and mass shootings.

### Turning Points in Europe
- **Battle of Stalingrad (1942-43)** — German 6th Army encircled and destroyed; 800,000 Axis casualties; marked the turning point on the Eastern Front
- **D-Day / Operation Overlord (6 June 1944)** — largest seaborne invasion in history; Allied forces landed on five beaches in Normandy, France; opened a second front

### Germany's Defeat
Allied forces advanced from west (after D-Day) and east (Soviet advances). Berlin fell in May 1945. Hitler committed suicide on 30 April 1945. Germany surrendered unconditionally on **8 May 1945 (V-E Day)**.

## The War in the Pacific

### Japan's Expansion
Japan invaded China (1937) and Southeast Asia, seeking resources and territory. The United States imposed oil embargoes in response.

### Pearl Harbor (7 December 1941)
Japan launched a surprise attack on the US naval base at Pearl Harbor, Hawaii. Over 2,400 Americans were killed and much of the Pacific Fleet was damaged. The United States declared war on Japan the following day.

### Turning Points in the Pacific
- **Battle of Midway (June 1942)** — US forces destroyed four Japanese aircraft carriers; ended Japanese naval superiority
- **Island-hopping campaign** — US forces bypassed heavily defended Japanese islands, capturing strategically important ones
- **Iwo Jima and Okinawa (1945)** — costly battles with massive casualties on both sides

### The Atomic Bombs and Japan's Surrender
The US dropped atomic bombs on **Hiroshima (6 August 1945)** and **Nagasaki (9 August 1945)**, killing approximately 110,000-210,000 people immediately and more from radiation effects. The Soviet Union also declared war on Japan. Japan surrendered on **15 August 1945 (V-J Day)**; formal surrender signed **2 September 1945**.

## Aftermath and Legacy

### Human Cost
- Estimated 70-85 million deaths — approximately 3% of the world's 1940 population
- More civilian deaths than military (bombing of cities, famine, genocide)
- Massive displacement of populations across Europe

### Political Changes
- Germany divided into four occupation zones; eventually split into West Germany (NATO) and East Germany (Soviet bloc)
- The United Nations established (1945) to replace the failed League of Nations
- Beginning of the **Cold War** between the United States and Soviet Union
- Decolonisation accelerated as European powers were weakened

### The Nuremberg Trials
Nazi leaders tried for war crimes and crimes against humanity. Established the principle that individuals could be held accountable under international law for such crimes.
