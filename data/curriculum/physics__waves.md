# Waves

## What is a Wave?
A wave is a disturbance that transfers energy from one place to another without transferring matter. The medium through which the wave travels oscillates but does not move along with the wave.

## Types of Waves

### Transverse Waves
Particles oscillate **perpendicular** to the direction of wave travel.
- Examples: light waves, water surface waves, waves on a string
- Can travel through vacuum (electromagnetic waves)

### Longitudinal Waves
Particles oscillate **parallel** to the direction of wave travel, creating compressions and rarefactions.
- Examples: sound waves, seismic P-waves
- Require a medium to travel through

## Wave Properties

### Amplitude (A)
Maximum displacement of a particle from its equilibrium position.
- Measured in metres
- Related to energy: greater amplitude = more energy

### Wavelength (λ — lambda)
Distance between two consecutive points in phase (e.g., two crests or two troughs).
- Measured in metres

### Frequency (f)
Number of complete oscillations per second.
- Measured in Hertz (Hz)
- 1 Hz = 1 cycle per second

### Period (T)
Time for one complete oscillation.
- T = 1/f
- Measured in seconds

### Wave Speed (v)
**v = fλ** (wave speed = frequency × wavelength)
- Speed of sound in air ≈ 340 m/s (at 20°C)
- Speed of light in vacuum = 3 × 10⁸ m/s

## Sound Waves

### Properties of Sound
- Longitudinal wave
- Requires a medium (cannot travel in vacuum)
- Speed depends on medium: faster in solids > liquids > gases
- Frequency determines pitch; amplitude determines loudness

### The Decibel Scale
Sound intensity is measured in decibels (dB).
- 0 dB: threshold of hearing
- 60 dB: normal conversation
- 120 dB: threshold of pain
- Logarithmic scale: 10 dB increase = 10× more intense

### Doppler Effect
The apparent change in frequency when the source and observer are moving relative to each other.
- Source moving toward observer: higher pitch (frequency increases)
- Source moving away from observer: lower pitch (frequency decreases)
- Examples: ambulance siren, red/blue shift of stars

## The Electromagnetic Spectrum
Electromagnetic (EM) waves are transverse waves that do not require a medium. All travel at the speed of light in vacuum (c = 3 × 10⁸ m/s).

From lowest to highest frequency (longest to shortest wavelength):

| Type | Wavelength | Uses |
|---|---|---|
| Radio waves | > 1 m | Broadcasting, WiFi |
| Microwaves | 1 mm - 1 m | Cooking, radar |
| Infrared | 700 nm - 1 mm | Thermal imaging, TV remotes |
| Visible light | 400 - 700 nm | Vision |
| Ultraviolet | 10 - 400 nm | Sterilisation, sunburn |
| X-rays | 0.01 - 10 nm | Medical imaging |
| Gamma rays | < 0.01 nm | Cancer treatment, sterilisation |

## Wave Behaviour

### Reflection
Wave bounces off a surface. Angle of incidence = angle of reflection.

### Refraction
Wave changes direction when it passes from one medium to another due to a change in speed.
- Light bends toward normal when entering a denser medium (slows down)
- Light bends away from normal when entering a less dense medium (speeds up)
- Snell's Law: n₁ sin θ₁ = n₂ sin θ₂

### Diffraction
Waves spread out when passing through a gap or around an obstacle.
- Most significant when gap size ≈ wavelength
- Explains why sound can be heard around corners but light cannot

### Interference
When two waves overlap, they combine:
- **Constructive interference** — waves in phase; amplitudes add → louder/brighter
- **Destructive interference** — waves out of phase; amplitudes cancel → quieter/darker

### Standing Waves
Formed when two waves of equal frequency and amplitude travel in opposite directions.
- **Nodes** — points of no displacement
- **Antinodes** — points of maximum displacement
- Natural frequencies at which standing waves form are called **harmonics**
