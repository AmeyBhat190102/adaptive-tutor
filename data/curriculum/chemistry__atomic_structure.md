# Atomic Structure

## The Atom
All matter is made of atoms. An atom is the smallest unit of an element that retains the chemical properties of that element.

## Subatomic Particles

| Particle | Symbol | Charge | Mass (amu) | Location |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| Proton | p⁺ | +1 | 1 | Nucleus |
| Neutron | n | 0 | 1 | Nucleus |
| Electron | e⁻ | -1 | 1/1836 | Electron shells |

### The Nucleus
The nucleus is at the centre of the atom and contains protons and neutrons (collectively called **nucleons**). It is extremely small but contains almost all the mass of the atom.

### Electrons
Electrons occupy regions of space called **electron shells** (or energy levels) around the nucleus. They have negligible mass compared to protons and neutrons.

## Atomic Number and Mass Number

**Atomic number (Z)** = number of protons
- Defines the element — all atoms of the same element have the same atomic number
- In a neutral atom: number of protons = number of electrons

**Mass number (A)** = number of protons + number of neutrons
- Number of neutrons = A - Z

**Notation:** ᴬzX, where X is the element symbol
- ¹²₆C: carbon with 6 protons and 6 neutrons
- ²³₁₁Na: sodium with 11 protons and 12 neutrons

## Isotopes
Atoms of the same element with different numbers of neutrons.
- Same atomic number (same element), different mass number
- Examples: ¹²C, ¹³C, ¹⁴C (carbon isotopes)
- ¹⁴C is radioactive (used in radiocarbon dating)
- Relative atomic mass is the weighted average of all isotopes

## Electron Configuration

### Energy Levels (Shells)
Electrons occupy shells at increasing distances from the nucleus:
- Shell 1 (n=1): maximum 2 electrons
- Shell 2 (n=2): maximum 8 electrons
- Shell 3 (n=3): maximum 18 electrons
- Shell 4 (n=4): maximum 32 electrons

**Rule:** Fill shells from lowest energy first.

**Examples:**
- Hydrogen (Z=1): 1
- Carbon (Z=6): 2, 4
- Sodium (Z=11): 2, 8, 1
- Chlorine (Z=17): 2, 8, 7
- Calcium (Z=20): 2, 8, 8, 2

### Subshells (s, p, d, f)
More precisely, electrons occupy subshells within each shell:
- s subshell: holds 2 electrons
- p subshell: holds 6 electrons
- d subshell: holds 10 electrons
- f subshell: holds 14 electrons

**Order of filling:** 1s, 2s, 2p, 3s, 3p, 4s, 3d, 4p...

**Example — Iron (Z=26):**
1s² 2s² 2p⁶ 3s² 3p⁶ 4s² 3d⁶

### Valence Electrons
Electrons in the outermost shell. These are the electrons involved in chemical bonding.
- Group 1 elements: 1 valence electron
- Group 7 elements: 7 valence electrons
- Noble gases: 8 valence electrons (full outer shell — very stable)

## The Periodic Table

### Organisation
- **Periods** (rows): elements in the same period have the same number of electron shells
- **Groups** (columns): elements in the same group have the same number of valence electrons and similar chemical properties

### Key Groups
- **Group 1 (Alkali metals):** 1 valence electron, highly reactive, form +1 ions
- **Group 2 (Alkaline earth metals):** 2 valence electrons, form +2 ions
- **Group 7 (Halogens):** 7 valence electrons, highly reactive non-metals, form -1 ions
- **Group 0 (Noble gases):** 8 valence electrons (full outer shell), very unreactive
- **Transition metals:** d-block elements, variable oxidation states, form coloured compounds

### Trends in the Periodic Table
- **Atomic radius:** increases down a group (more shells); decreases across a period (more protons pull electrons closer)
- **Ionisation energy:** energy to remove an electron. Increases across a period; decreases down a group
- **Electronegativity:** ability to attract electrons in a bond. Increases across a period; decreases down a group (Fluorine is most electronegative)
- **Metallic character:** decreases across a period; increases down a group

## Ions
When an atom gains or loses electrons, it becomes an **ion**.
- **Cation** (positive ion): atom lost electrons — Na → Na⁺ + e⁻
- **Anion** (negative ion): atom gained electrons — Cl + e⁻ → Cl⁻

Ions have the electron configuration of the nearest noble gas.
