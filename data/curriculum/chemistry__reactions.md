# Chemical Reactions

## What is a Chemical Reaction?
A chemical reaction is a process where reactants are converted into products through the breaking and forming of chemical bonds. Matter is conserved — atoms are rearranged, not created or destroyed.

## Balancing Chemical Equations

### Why Balance?
The Law of Conservation of Mass states that mass is conserved in a reaction. A balanced equation has equal numbers of each atom on both sides.

### How to Balance
1. Write the unbalanced equation
2. Count atoms of each element on each side
3. Add coefficients (numbers in front of formulas) to balance — never change subscripts
4. Check your work

**Example:** Burning methane
Unbalanced: CH₄ + O₂ → CO₂ + H₂O
Count: C: 1=1 ✓, H: 4≠2, O: 2≠3
Balance H: CH₄ + O₂ → CO₂ + 2H₂O
Recount O: 2≠4, fix: CH₄ + 2O₂ → CO₂ + 2H₂O ✓

## Types of Chemical Reactions

### 1. Synthesis (Combination)
A + B → AB
Two or more substances combine to form one product.
- 2Na + Cl₂ → 2NaCl
- CaO + H₂O → Ca(OH)₂

### 2. Decomposition
AB → A + B
One substance breaks down into two or more simpler substances.
- 2H₂O₂ → 2H₂O + O₂
- CaCO₃ → CaO + CO₂ (thermal decomposition)

### 3. Single Displacement (Substitution)
A + BC → AC + B
A more reactive element displaces a less reactive element from a compound.
- Zn + CuSO₄ → ZnSO₄ + Cu
- Uses the reactivity series to predict if reaction occurs

### 4. Double Displacement (Metathesis)
AB + CD → AD + CB
Ions from two compounds swap partners. Usually forms a precipitate, water, or gas.
- AgNO₃ + NaCl → AgCl↓ + NaNO₃ (precipitate formation)
- HCl + NaOH → NaCl + H₂O (neutralisation)

### 5. Combustion
Fuel + O₂ → CO₂ + H₂O (complete combustion)
- CH₄ + 2O₂ → CO₂ + 2H₂O
- Incomplete combustion produces CO (carbon monoxide) and/or C (soot)

### 6. Acid-Base (Neutralisation)
Acid + Base → Salt + Water
- HCl + NaOH → NaCl + H₂O
- H₂SO₄ + 2KOH → K₂SO₄ + 2H₂O

## Stoichiometry

### The Mole
The mole (mol) is the SI unit for amount of substance.
1 mole = 6.022 × 10²³ particles (Avogadro's number)

**Molar mass** = mass of one mole of a substance = sum of atomic masses (in g/mol)
- Molar mass of H₂O = 2(1) + 16 = 18 g/mol
- Molar mass of NaCl = 23 + 35.5 = 58.5 g/mol

**Converting:**
- Moles = mass / molar mass (n = m/M)
- Particles = moles × 6.022 × 10²³

### Mole Ratios
Coefficients in a balanced equation give mole ratios.
For: N₂ + 3H₂ → 2NH₃
- 1 mol N₂ reacts with 3 mol H₂ to produce 2 mol NH₃

**Example:** How many moles of NH₃ are produced from 0.5 mol N₂?
0.5 mol N₂ × (2 mol NH₃ / 1 mol N₂) = 1 mol NH₃

### Limiting Reagent
The reactant that runs out first — limits how much product can form.

**Steps:**
1. Convert both reactants to moles
2. Use mole ratio to find how much of one reactant is needed to react with all of the other
3. The reactant that is insufficient is the limiting reagent
4. Calculate product based on limiting reagent

### Percentage Yield
Yield (%) = (actual yield / theoretical yield) × 100%
- Theoretical yield: calculated from stoichiometry (maximum possible)
- Actual yield: experimentally measured (always ≤ theoretical)
- Losses due to: incomplete reaction, side reactions, product lost in transfer

## Reaction Rates

### Factors Affecting Reaction Rate
1. **Concentration** — more particles per unit volume → more frequent collisions → faster rate
2. **Temperature** — higher temperature → particles move faster → more energetic and frequent collisions
3. **Surface area** — smaller particle size → greater exposed area → more collisions (e.g., powder reacts faster than lump)
4. **Catalyst** — provides an alternative reaction pathway with lower activation energy → faster rate without being consumed
5. **Pressure** (gases only) — higher pressure → higher concentration of gas particles

### Collision Theory
Reactions occur when:
1. Particles collide with each other
2. Collision has sufficient energy (≥ activation energy)
3. Particles have correct orientation

**Activation energy (Eₐ)** — minimum energy required for a successful collision

### Energy Diagrams
- **Exothermic reaction:** products have less energy than reactants; ΔH is negative; releases heat
- **Endothermic reaction:** products have more energy than reactants; ΔH is positive; absorbs heat

## Acids and Bases

### Definitions
**Arrhenius:** Acid produces H⁺ in water; base produces OH⁻ in water
**Brønsted-Lowry:** Acid is a proton (H⁺) donor; base is a proton acceptor

### pH Scale
pH = -log[H⁺]
- pH 0-7: acidic (lower = more acidic)
- pH 7: neutral
- pH 7-14: basic/alkaline (higher = more basic)

[H⁺][OH⁻] = 10⁻¹⁴ at 25°C

### Strong vs Weak Acids
- **Strong acids:** fully dissociate in water (HCl, H₂SO₄, HNO₃)
- **Weak acids:** partially dissociate; establish equilibrium (CH₃COOH, carbonic acid)
