# Photosynthesis

## Overview
Photosynthesis is the process by which plants, algae, and some bacteria convert light energy into chemical energy stored as glucose. It is the foundation of nearly all food chains on Earth and the primary source of oxygen in our atmosphere.

The overall equation for photosynthesis is:
6CO₂ + 6H₂O + light energy → C₆H₁₂O₆ + 6O₂

## Where Photosynthesis Occurs
Photosynthesis takes place inside **chloroplasts**, organelles found in plant cells and algae. Chloroplasts contain a green pigment called **chlorophyll**, which absorbs light energy — primarily in the red and blue wavelengths — and reflects green light, which is why plants appear green.

Chloroplasts have two main regions:
- **Thylakoids** — flattened membrane sacs arranged in stacks called grana, where the light-dependent reactions occur
- **Stroma** — the fluid-filled space surrounding the thylakoids, where the light-independent reactions occur

## Stage 1 — Light-Dependent Reactions
The light-dependent reactions (also called the light reactions) occur in the **thylakoid membranes**.

### What happens:
1. Chlorophyll and other pigments in **Photosystem II** absorb sunlight
2. The energy is used to split water molecules — a process called **photolysis**:
   2H₂O → 4H⁺ + 4e⁻ + O₂
3. Oxygen is released as a byproduct (this is the oxygen we breathe)
4. Electrons pass through the **electron transport chain**, releasing energy used to pump H⁺ ions across the thylakoid membrane
5. This creates a concentration gradient that drives **ATP synthase** to produce **ATP**
6. Electrons reach **Photosystem I**, where they are re-energised by light and used to reduce NADP⁺ to **NADPH**

### Products of the light reactions:
- ATP (energy currency)
- NADPH (electron carrier)
- Oxygen (released into atmosphere)

## Stage 2 — The Calvin Cycle (Light-Independent Reactions)
The Calvin cycle occurs in the **stroma** and does not directly require light, but depends on the ATP and NADPH produced by the light reactions.

### Steps:
1. **Carbon fixation** — CO₂ from the atmosphere is attached to a 5-carbon molecule called **RuBP** (ribulose bisphosphate) by the enzyme **RuBisCO**, producing an unstable 6-carbon compound that immediately splits into two 3-carbon molecules called **3-PGA** (3-phosphoglycerate)
2. **Reduction** — ATP and NADPH are used to convert 3-PGA into **G3P** (glyceraldehyde-3-phosphate), a simple sugar
3. **Regeneration of RuBP** — most G3P molecules are used to regenerate RuBP using ATP, keeping the cycle going. One G3P molecule exits the cycle for every 3 CO₂ molecules fixed

### Products:
- G3P (used to build glucose, amino acids, fatty acids)
- ADP and NADP⁺ (returned to the light reactions to be recharged)

## Factors Affecting the Rate of Photosynthesis
The rate of photosynthesis is affected by:

1. **Light intensity** — increasing light intensity increases the rate up to a saturation point, after which other factors become limiting
2. **CO₂ concentration** — more CO₂ increases the rate of carbon fixation, up to a point
3. **Temperature** — photosynthesis involves enzymes (especially RuBisCO), so rate increases with temperature up to an optimum (~25-35°C), then falls sharply as enzymes denature
4. **Water availability** — water is a raw material; severe water stress causes stomata to close, reducing CO₂ entry

## C3, C4, and CAM Plants
Different plants have evolved variations of photosynthesis:
- **C3 plants** (wheat, rice, trees) — use standard Calvin cycle; first stable product is a 3-carbon molecule
- **C4 plants** (corn, sugarcane) — pre-fix CO₂ in mesophyll cells to reduce photorespiration in hot conditions
- **CAM plants** (cacti, succulents) — open stomata only at night to conserve water in arid environments

## Importance of Photosynthesis
- Produces oxygen for aerobic respiration
- Fixes carbon from CO₂ into organic compounds, forming the base of food chains
- Drives the global carbon cycle
- Source of fossil fuels (ancient photosynthetic organisms)
