# Cell Biology

## The Cell Theory
The cell theory, established in the 19th century, states:
1. All living organisms are made of one or more cells
2. The cell is the basic unit of life
3. All cells arise from pre-existing cells

## Prokaryotic vs Eukaryotic Cells

### Prokaryotic Cells
- No membrane-bound nucleus — DNA floats freely in the cytoplasm
- Smaller (~1-10 μm)
- No membrane-bound organelles
- Examples: bacteria, archaea
- Contain ribosomes, cell membrane, and cell wall (usually)

### Eukaryotic Cells
- Membrane-bound nucleus containing DNA
- Larger (~10-100 μm)
- Many membrane-bound organelles
- Examples: animal cells, plant cells, fungi, protists

## Cell Structure — Animal Cell

### Nucleus
The control centre of the cell. Contains DNA organised into chromosomes. The **nuclear envelope** (double membrane) surrounds it, with **nuclear pores** allowing transport of molecules in and out. The **nucleolus** inside the nucleus is where ribosomes are assembled.

### Mitochondria
Often called the "powerhouse of the cell." Site of cellular respiration — produces ATP from glucose and oxygen. Has a double membrane; the inner membrane is folded into **cristae** to increase surface area. Contains its own DNA (evidence of endosymbiotic origin).

### Ribosomes
Sites of protein synthesis. Found free in the cytoplasm or attached to the rough endoplasmic reticulum. Made of rRNA and proteins.

### Endoplasmic Reticulum (ER)
- **Rough ER** — studded with ribosomes; synthesises and processes proteins destined for secretion or membrane insertion
- **Smooth ER** — no ribosomes; involved in lipid synthesis, detoxification, and calcium storage

### Golgi Apparatus
Receives proteins from the rough ER, modifies them (e.g., adding sugar groups), packages them into vesicles, and sends them to their destination (secretion, lysosomes, or membrane).

### Lysosomes
Membrane-bound sacs containing digestive enzymes. Break down waste materials, cellular debris, and foreign invaders. Maintain acidic pH (~4.8) to activate enzymes.

### Cell Membrane (Plasma Membrane)
Phospholipid bilayer with embedded proteins. Controls what enters and exits the cell. The **fluid mosaic model** describes it as a fluid structure with proteins embedded throughout.

## Cell Structure — Plant Cell (Additional Structures)

### Cell Wall
Rigid outer layer made of **cellulose** (in plants). Provides structural support and protection. Located outside the cell membrane. Freely permeable — does not control what passes through.

### Chloroplasts
Site of photosynthesis. Contains chlorophyll. Has inner and outer membranes and its own DNA (endosymbiotic origin, like mitochondria).

### Central Vacuole
Large, fluid-filled sac occupying up to 90% of a mature plant cell's volume. Stores water, nutrients, and waste. When full, pushes against the cell wall creating **turgor pressure**, keeping the plant rigid.

### Plasmodesmata
Channels connecting adjacent plant cells, allowing communication and transport between cells.

## The Cell Membrane in Detail

### Phospholipid Bilayer
Each phospholipid has a hydrophilic (water-loving) head and two hydrophobic (water-fearing) fatty acid tails. In water, they spontaneously form a bilayer with heads facing outward and tails facing inward.

### Membrane Proteins
- **Integral proteins** — span the entire membrane (e.g., channel proteins, carrier proteins)
- **Peripheral proteins** — attached to the surface (e.g., cell recognition receptors)

### Transport Across the Membrane
- **Passive transport** — no energy required; moves down concentration gradient
  - Simple diffusion (small, nonpolar molecules: O₂, CO₂)
  - Facilitated diffusion (polar/charged molecules via channel or carrier proteins)
  - Osmosis (water via aquaporins)
- **Active transport** — requires ATP; moves against concentration gradient
  - Sodium-potassium pump (Na⁺/K⁺ ATPase)
  - Endocytosis (cell engulfs material)
  - Exocytosis (cell expels material)

## Mitosis — Cell Division for Growth and Repair

Mitosis produces two genetically identical daughter cells from one parent cell. It is used for growth, repair, and asexual reproduction.

### Stages of Mitosis:
1. **Prophase** — chromatin condenses into visible chromosomes; mitotic spindle begins to form; nuclear envelope breaks down
2. **Metaphase** — chromosomes align along the cell's equator (metaphase plate); spindle fibres attach to centromeres
3. **Anaphase** — sister chromatids are pulled to opposite poles of the cell
4. **Telophase** — nuclear envelopes reform around each set of chromosomes; chromosomes decondense
5. **Cytokinesis** — cytoplasm divides, producing two daughter cells

### Mitosis vs Meiosis
| Feature | Mitosis | Meiosis |
|---|---|---|
| Purpose | Growth, repair | Sexual reproduction |
| Divisions | 1 | 2 |
| Daughter cells | 2 | 4 |
| Chromosome number | Same as parent (diploid) | Half of parent (haploid) |
| Genetic variation | None (identical) | Yes (crossing over, random assortment) |

## Cell Cycle
The cell cycle has two main phases:
- **Interphase** (G1, S, G2) — cell grows, replicates DNA, prepares for division
- **Mitotic phase** — mitosis + cytokinesis

**Checkpoints** monitor the cell cycle:
- G1 checkpoint — checks if cell is large enough and DNA is undamaged
- G2 checkpoint — checks if DNA replication was completed accurately
- Spindle checkpoint — checks if chromosomes are properly attached

Cancer results from mutations that disable these checkpoints, causing uncontrolled cell division.
